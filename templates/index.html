In the 1970s, the famous Stanford marshmallow experiment found that children able to delay gratification (demonstrated by waiting 15 minutes to get two treats instead of one) would later be more academically capable and socially competent, earn higher SAT scores, and have better stress management.

It was a critical study, showing that self-control is a fundamental skill which, if developed early, leads to lifelong success. Essentially, it proved that if you worked hard and had enough willpower, you would be successful.

But it's not because you're unable to wait for an ad to end to receive 100 dollars. Instead it was because you didn't believe it would happen if you had waited. This is the exact finding of a paper titled "Rational Snacking", one of many that revisited the Stanford Marshmallow Experiment.

In the Rational Snacking study, researchers split 28 children into one of two groups: reliable and unreliable. In both cases, the child was given a jar of crayons. The researcher told the child that if they waited 2.5 minutes without opening the jar, they would be back with a box of cool art supplies.

The jar was tightly sealed, so every child chose to wait.

In the reliable condition, the researcher returned with a large tray of cool art supplies.

In the unreliable condition, the researcher returned empty-handed, explaining that there weren't any other art supplies after all, and helped the child open the jar.

The same process was repeated with stickers instead of art supplies.

After being shown evidence that the researcher was reliable or unreliable, the child was presented with the marshmallow task: either immediately eat the marshmallow, or wait until the researcher got back with another.

Take a minute to consider - which group of children would you expect to have waited longer? And by how much?

In the reliable condition, 9 out of 14 children (64.3%) waited the full 15 minutes.

In the unreliable condition, only 1 out of 14 children (7.1%) waited the full 15 minutes.

(Unrelated to the findings of the experiment, every child was debriefed and received 3 marshmallows after the experiment, regardless of whether they waited or not.)

These findings show that self-control is not the only factor behind the marshmallow experiment - children rationally chose not to wait based on their previous experiences.

But that doesn't address the original Stanford experiment. What are the factors behind it, and why would it be correlated with success later in life? There's something more than just whether or not the child believes they'll receive another marshmallow.

In another follow-up experiment, researchers repeated the original marshmallow experiment with a much larger sample size, this time checking for potential confounding variables.

Without controlling for anything, the initial correlation between waiting and achievement at age 15 was moderate and statistically significant (β = 0.24, p < .001).

When controls for child background and home environment were added, the correlation fell substantially (β = 0.08, p = .016).

Statistically significant factors included whether the home environment was organized and had learning materials, whether parents modeled good behavior and responded to the child's needs, and whether the child had diverse daily experiences.

When controls for cognitive ability were added, the correlation became nonsignificant (β = 0.05, p = .140).

The researchers also found that the marshmallow test had a higher correlation with cognitive ability than with self-control, attention, or impulsivity.

Contrary to the initial findings, this study shows that basic cognitive ability is a stronger predictor of future success than pure self-control.

This means children who grow up with more exposure to learning opportunities and environmental stability are more likely to understand the link between their actions and outcomes - making it easier for them to wait.

While children don't have much control over their learning opportunities and environment, later on in life there are cognitive skills and ways of framing situations that make delaying gratification - and making better decisions in general - easier.

In fact, the point of the original Stanford marshmallow experiment is highly misunderstood. It wasn't just to see if children with early self-control would go on to live better lives; it was a part of a series of experiments that came before it investigating mental strategies for the purpose of delaying gratification. The researchers found children could wait a long time if they believed they really would get their treat, weren't thinking about the marshmallow, and occupied themselves with happy thoughts or toys.

It's undeniable that the ability to exert self-control and delay gratification is an important skill to have in life, regardless of what early experiences may indicate. Countless decisions in our daily lives require us to let go of immediate comfort to work towards long-term goals.

Going to sleep early instead of staying up playing video games. Talking with someone new instead of staying comfortable. Sitting down to work instead of procrastinating. Being able to make these decisions is becoming more and more important, with YouTube, video games, and social media becoming increasingly accessible and engaging.

And so, as adults, how can we improve our ability to delay gratification?

Start with small risks:

The trouble with delayed gratification is that the reward you get at the end may not be worth the trouble you go through. It may be really risky to work on something for years with it potentially not paying off. And while sometimes our decision to not delay gratification is rational, other times it’s from not having enough experience with delaying gratification to feel comfortable with it.

So take small risks, and build up that trust. Try just going to sleep early or taking a walk outside, and if you end up feeling better from it, make a mental note that this is the result of you choosing to do something for your future self.

Try a new environment:

Changing your surroundings removes familiar cues that pull you toward immediate rewards. A library, a cafe, a different room - small shifts can make a big difference.

Find ways to enjoy the process:

And finally, it's both reasonable and supported by studies to think that it's much harder to delay gratification if the process of delaying gratification is unenjoyable. It's much easier to not choose immediate rewards if we are doing something enjoyable or engaging.

So try finding ways to make the process enjoyable or new. For example, if going to the gym is not very fun, try going with friends, or doing some other kind of exercise that's more engaging. This has the added benefit of making delaying gratification less risky, since you are already doing something that you like.

And that's just a few of them. There are a lot more strategies out there, you just have to find the ones that work for you.

So in conclusion, self-control and willpower are very important abilities to have on hand, but also need to be used rationally. Additionally, self-control is dependant on a stable and predictable environment, and in children, the modeling and resources to learn its value. Maintaining an environment that rewards delaying gratification maintains the ability to exert self-control.

So next time you are sitting at a table looking at a marshmallow, instead of just pushing through the temptation with pure willpower, it's a lot easier to just get up and take a walk.
